# Atlas Bar Library — Canonical Doctrine

**Status: LOCKED.** This document defines the Atlas Bar Library for digital professionals.
It is subordinate to `ATLAS_OS.md` and `CONSTRAINT_DESIGN_MANUAL.md` and must never
contradict them. Every entry below is a survivor of the **Seven Tests** (Manual §2) and a
member of the Constraint Library (`ATLAS_OS.md` §4). The Bar Library exists to make the
**Bar** — the falsifiable definition of "good" — and its **difficulty ladder** explicit for
each constraint, so reps can be measured and tightened.

**Every bar in this library is, without exception:**
- **Falsifiable** — an artifact can fail it.
- **Externally anchored** — passing is confirmed by a real receiver (a prospect, an
  engineer, an exec, a peer), never by Atlas's say-so.
- **Self-securing** — the only way to pass is to actually have the capability.
- **Recognizable** — the improvement can be seen, read, and credited by someone with
  pricing power.
- **30-day movable** — deliberate corrected practice can move it within one Sprint.
- **Qualitative** — expressed as conditions and hit / partial / miss. **No scores, /100,
  bands, market value, or salary anywhere.**

The **difficulty ladder** for every constraint follows the universal arc (`ATLAS_OS.md` §5):
**Rung 1 Present → Rung 2 Exclusive/Falsifiable → Rung 3 Unaided → Rung 4 Confirmed in the
wild.** The bar rises by *clearing*, never by elapsed time — there are no weeks here.

Reps are **always on the user's own real work** — never exercises, lessons, or modules.

# Part 1 — The V1 Seed Constraints

## Marketer

### M1 — Generic Positioning

**Why it matters.** In an AI-flooded market, undifferentiated messaging is invisible and
instantly replaceable by any tool. The ability to make a claim *only you* can make is the
scarce capability a marketer is kept for.

**Failure Mode.** Every claim ("data-driven", "customer-centric", "results-focused") is
equally true of three competitors; no prospect can finish "why you, not them."

**The Bar.** A prospect repeats "why you, not them" in one sentence, and it is demonstrably
false of three named competitors.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — Distinct:** a reader can say, in their own words, what you do.
- **Rung 2 — Exclusive:** the claim is false of three named competitors and is provable from
  the work, not a category truism.
- **Rung 3 — Unaided:** you produce a passing claim cold, on a product/segment you haven't
  worked, with no prompts.
- **Rung 4 — Confirmed in the wild:** on a live launch asset, a real prospect plays "why you"
  back to you, unprompted.

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Rewrite one live claim from your own work; save it as the baseline (it will be
  generic — that's the point).
- **Intermediate:** Rewrite the claims on one real campaign until each names the three
  competitors it excludes; test "who does this rule out?" on a colleague.
- **Independence:** Write a full positioning for a different product or segment, unaided,
  under "one sentence, no jargon."
- **Proof:** Ship a complete positioning/page for a real launch; have a real prospect repeat
  "why you" in their own words.

**Proof.** A live positioning or page for real work that a real prospect or colleague
distinguishes from three named competitors, unaided.

**Recognition.** You show the original and rewritten positioning to whoever prices your work:
"I can now write positioning a prospect repeats back and that no competitor could claim,"
evidenced by the launch asset and the prospect's playback.

**Should Decline If.** The product genuinely has no differentiator (a product/strategy
problem, not a capability); no access to real positioning work to rep on; the user can't name
real competitors to test exclusion; the real block is confidence or visibility, not the
ability to differentiate.

### M2 — Activity Reporting

**Why it matters.** A marketer who can't tie work to a business outcome is the first cut in a
downturn and the easiest to replace with a tool. Outcome-legibility is what makes the work
defensible.

**Failure Mode.** Updates list outputs — emails sent, posts shipped, campaigns launched — and
never name the business metric moved or a falsifiable hypothesis about one.

**The Bar.** Every claim of work ties to a business outcome, or to an explicit falsifiable
hypothesis about one, in the language a founder or finance lead uses.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — Named outcome:** each piece of work names the outcome it targets.
- **Rung 2 — Falsifiable link:** the outcome is stated as a hypothesis a skeptic could
  disprove, and it survives "so what moved?"
- **Rung 3 — Unaided framing:** you frame a new initiative outcome-first, cold, including
  honest attribution and what can't be attributed.
- **Rung 4 — Accepted by a pricer:** a founder or finance lead accepts a real report that
  leads with outcome without asking "so what?"

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Rewrite last week's status update to lead with outcomes; save as baseline.
- **Intermediate:** Reframe one real campaign as a business case — the outcome it targets and
  the hypothesis behind it.
- **Independence:** Present a real result with its metric and an honest read of attribution,
  unaided.
- **Proof:** Produce a full outcome-framed plan or report for a real initiative that a founder
  accepts without "so what moved?"

**Proof.** A real report or brief that leads with a business outcome and survives a skeptical
founder or finance reader.

**Recognition.** You bring the outcome-framed report to whoever prices your work: "I now
report what my work moves in the business, not just what I shipped."

**Should Decline If.** No access to outcome data and can't get it (a data-access
circumstance); the business has no measurable outcomes to tie to; the real block is
data-analysis skill, not outcome framing.

## Founder

### F1 — Unclear Winning Narrative

**Why it matters.** A founder who can't tell the story can't raise, recruit, or sell. The
narrative is the founder's primary instrument of leverage, and no one can do it for them.

**Failure Mode.** The pitch is a feature list or a meandering origin story; a listener can't
repeat the problem, why-now, or why-this-team-wins.

**The Bar.** A naive listener repeats the problem, why-now, and why-this-team-wins in three
sentences, unprompted.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — Repeatable thesis:** a listener plays back the core problem.
- **Rung 2 — Full thesis:** they repeat problem + why-now + why-team in three sentences,
  accurately — not a vague gist.
- **Rung 3 — Survives objection, unaided:** you deliver it cold and handle the single hardest
  objection without it collapsing.
- **Rung 4 — Lands in the room:** in a real high-stakes setting (investor, recruit, customer),
  a cold listener plays the thesis back and acts on it.

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Pitch to two real people; ask each to repeat it back; the gap between intent and
  playback is your baseline.
- **Intermediate:** Write the three-sentence version; test on real people until one repeats it
  accurately.
- **Independence:** Deliver the full arc to a naive listener and handle the hardest objection,
  unaided.
- **Proof:** Deliver it in a real high-stakes context where a cold listener repeats the thesis
  back.

**Proof.** A real pitch — deck, email, or recorded conversation — where a cold, first-time
listener accurately plays back problem + why-now + why-team.

**Recognition.** The narrative itself does the recognizing: deployed to investors, recruits,
or customers who repeat it accurately and act on it.

**Should Decline If.** The strategy itself is unclear (a strategy problem, not a storytelling
capability); no real audience to pitch during the sprint; the real block is stage confidence,
not the narrative.

### F2 — Cannot Say No

**Why it matters.** The founder's focus is the company's focus. A founder who can't cut
becomes the bottleneck on everything; the ability to kill good options to protect the one bet
is what makes a founder hard to replace as a decision-maker.

**Failure Mode.** Nine priorities, nothing explicitly killed; every new idea gets a "yes"; the
plan reads as a wish list.

**The Bar.** Names the single most important thing and what was explicitly killed to protect
it, with the reasoning — and defends it to the team.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — One priority named:** states the single most important thing this period.
- **Rung 2 — Explicit kill:** names what was killed to protect it, with the reasoning and the
  cost stated.
- **Rung 3 — Costly cut, unaided:** makes a real cut that hurts (kills a live project or
  feature) and defends it cold.
- **Rung 4 — Defended and held:** a real plan with one bet and explicit non-goals, defended to
  the team and held over time.

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Cut this week to one priority and name what's killed; baseline.
- **Intermediate:** Make a real cut that costs something — kill a live project or feature — and
  write the reasoning.
- **Independence:** Re-plan a period around one bet with explicit non-goals, unaided.
- **Proof:** Defend the one-bet plan and its killed alternatives to the team, and hold it.

**Proof.** A real plan or decision naming the one bet and the killed alternatives, defended to
the team — their acceptance is the validation.

**Recognition.** The team and board see a founder who sets and holds focus: the plan names the
bet and the non-goals, and the founder defends them.

**Should Decline If.** The real block is the founder lacks the authority to cut (a
circumstance); there's no live set of competing priorities to rep on; the constraint is
actually strategy (which bet is right) rather than the act of cutting.

## Product Manager

### P1 — Weak Problem Framing

**Why it matters.** A PM's leverage is framing. A mushy problem wastes engineering and
produces unfalsifiable success; sharp framing is the capability that makes a PM hard to
replace.

**Failure Mode.** The PRD opens with a solution, the "problem" is a feature request in
disguise, non-goals are absent, and success is not falsifiable.

**The Bar.** The problem is stated so sharply that the solution feels obvious, non-goals are
explicit, and success is a falsifiable metric.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — Problem, not solution:** the statement describes a problem, not a feature.
- **Rung 2 — Sharp and bounded:** the solution feels obvious, non-goals are explicit and
  load-bearing, and success is falsifiable.
- **Rung 3 — Cold framing, unaided:** you frame a genuinely new, ambiguous ask to the bar,
  unaided.
- **Rung 4 — Validated by an eng-lead:** a real PRD an engineering lead confirms is unusually
  clear on problem + non-goals + success.

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Rewrite one live PRD's problem statement; baseline.
- **Intermediate:** Rewrite one live PRD's problem to the bar; test "is the solution obvious
  now?" on an engineer.
- **Independence:** Frame a new, ambiguous ask cold — explicit non-goals and falsifiable
  success.
- **Proof:** Write a full PRD for a real upcoming initiative an eng-lead validates as
  unusually clear.

**Proof.** A real PRD an engineering lead confirms is unusually clear on problem, non-goals,
and falsifiable success.

**Recognition.** Your lead and stakeholders see problem statements where the solution is
obvious and success is falsifiable — the eng-lead's sign-off is the marker.

**Should Decline If.** You don't write or own problem docs in your role (no real reps); the
real block is stakeholder politics deciding priorities; the constraint is actually
prioritization (P2), not framing a single problem.

### P2 — Roadmap As Wishlist

**Why it matters.** PMs are paid to choose. An un-prioritized roadmap means the team optimizes
nothing; defensible trade-off reasoning is what makes a PM hard to replace as a decision-maker
rather than a backlog scribe.

**Failure Mode.** Everything ranked "high"; no trade-off reasoning; the PM can't defend why X
over Y beyond "a stakeholder asked."

**The Bar.** Every priority states the trade-off — what we're NOT doing and why — in
impact-vs-cost terms, defensibly.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — Ranked with reasons:** items are ordered, each with a stated reason.
- **Rung 2 — Explicit trade-offs:** each call names what it displaces and why, in
  impact-vs-cost terms.
- **Rung 3 — Re-prioritize under constraint, unaided:** given a new constraint (a cut budget,
  a deadline), you re-rank cold and defend it.
- **Rung 4 — Accepted by an exec:** a real roadmap an exec or eng-lead accepts as
  well-reasoned.

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Rank one real backlog with explicit trade-offs; baseline.
- **Intermediate:** Defend one ranking to a stakeholder; make one costly cut.
- **Independence:** Re-prioritize under a new constraint, unaided.
- **Proof:** Produce a full roadmap with trade-off logic an exec accepts as well-reasoned.

**Proof.** A real roadmap an exec or eng-lead accepts as well-reasoned — their acceptance
validates it.

**Recognition.** Execs see prioritization they can defend up the chain — explicit non-goals
and trade-offs, not a flat "everything's high."

**Should Decline If.** You don't own a roadmap or backlog (no reps); the real block is the org
won't let you cut (a circumstance / authority issue); the constraint is actually problem
framing (P1) upstream.

## Growth Operator

### G1 — Activity Not Experiments

**Why it matters.** Growth without falsifiable experiments is just spending. An operator who
can't run a real experiment can't compound learning and is indistinguishable from luck — and
luck is replaceable.

**Failure Mode.** Tactics launched with no stated hypothesis and no pre-set threshold; results
are "it kind of worked," with no learning extracted.

**The Bar.** Every growth action is a falsifiable experiment — hypothesis, metric, threshold,
and a hit/miss read committed before the result is known.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — Hypothesis stated:** the action names a hypothesis that could be wrong.
- **Rung 2 — Pre-registered:** a metric and pass threshold are set before launch, and the read
  is committed in advance.
- **Rung 3 — Clean isolation, unaided:** you design an experiment that isolates one variable
  and read it honestly, cold.
- **Rung 4 — Drives a decision, peer-accepted:** a real pre-registered experiment with an
  honest post-read a data-literate peer accepts as sound.

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Take one live tactic and try to write its hypothesis and threshold after the
  fact; the difficulty of doing so is the baseline.
- **Intermediate:** Convert one live tactic into a pre-registered experiment and launch it.
- **Independence:** Design a sharper experiment that isolates one variable, and read it
  honestly, unaided.
- **Proof:** Run a real experiment that drives a decision, with a pre-committed hit/miss
  verdict and the learning.

**Proof.** A real experiment document — pre-registered hypothesis and threshold plus an honest
post-read — that a data-literate peer accepts as sound.

**Recognition.** Whoever prices your work sees growth run as falsifiable experiments that
compound into learning, not one-off tactics — the pre-registered doc and verdict are the
marker.

**Should Decline If.** No access or traffic to run a real experiment in the sprint; the real
block is analytics or tooling, not experimental thinking; the constraint is actually metric
selection (G2), not rigor.

### G2 — Vanity Metrics

**Why it matters.** Chasing the wrong metric (signups over activation or retention) wastes the
whole funnel and the operator's credibility. The ability to anchor on the metric that maps to
value — and resist the flattering proxy — is what makes a growth operator trustworthy and hard
to replace.

**Failure Mode.** Celebrates clicks and signups while the metric that pays (activation,
retention, revenue) is flat or unmeasured; the causal chain to value is never stated.

**The Bar.** Every initiative names the one metric that maps to value, resists the vanity
proxy, and states the causal chain to revenue or retention.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — Value metric named:** the initiative names the metric that maps to value, not the
  proxy.
- **Rung 2 — Causal chain stated:** the path from the action to value is explicit and
  falsifiable, and the proxy is explicitly resisted.
- **Rung 3 — New funnel, unaided:** you map a new funnel to its value metric cold and redesign
  a tactic to move value, not the proxy.
- **Rung 4 — Plan anchored on value, defended:** a real growth plan anchored on the value
  metric with the causal chain defended to a skeptic.

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Re-pick the true value metric for one live initiative; baseline (notice how much
  was proxy).
- **Intermediate:** Map one funnel to its value metric; redesign a tactic to move value, not
  the proxy.
- **Independence:** Anchor a new initiative on its value metric, unaided, with the causal
  chain.
- **Proof:** Produce a real growth plan anchored on the value metric, causal chain defended.

**Proof.** A real growth plan or analysis anchored on the value metric with the causal chain
defended — a skeptic or peer accepts it.

**Recognition.** Leadership sees growth work anchored on what actually pays, with the causal
chain — not a vanity dashboard.

**Should Decline If.** The value metric is genuinely unmeasurable in the current stack (a
circumstance); the real block is data instrumentation, not metric judgment; the constraint is
actually experimental rigor (G1).

## AI Operator

### A1 — Spec Ambiguity

**Why it matters.** The AI operator's core leverage is precise specification. Vague specs
produce unreliable systems; an operator who only re-rolls until something looks right adds no
durable value over a casual user — the opposite of hard to replace.

**Failure Mode.** Prompts and specs are under-constrained; output varies run to run; failures
are handled by re-rolling rather than tightening the spec; the operator can't say why it
failed.

**The Bar.** A task is specified precisely enough that output is reliable across cases and any
failure is diagnosable to a specific gap in the spec, not to luck.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — Constrained and exampled:** the spec states explicit constraints and includes
  worked examples.
- **Rung 2 — Reliable across cases:** output is consistent across a set of real test cases.
- **Rung 3 — Failure-diagnosable, unaided:** when a new task fails, you trace it to a named
  spec gap, cold, and fix the spec rather than re-roll.
- **Rung 4 — Peer-trusted in production:** a real workflow whose reliability across cases a
  technical peer trusts.

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Run one live prompt several times; document the variance; baseline.
- **Intermediate:** Rewrite the spec with explicit constraints and worked examples; build a
  small set of real test cases; reach consistent output.
- **Independence:** Spec a genuinely new task to reliability, unaided; trace a failure to the
  spec.
- **Proof:** Ship a real, reliable AI workflow whose consistency is demonstrated across cases
  and trusted by a technical peer.

**Proof.** A real AI workflow plus its test cases showing reliable output, with failures
traced to the spec, accepted by a technical peer.

**Recognition.** Whoever prices your work sees AI tasks specified to measurable reliability
with diagnosable failures — the working, peer-trusted workflow is the marker.

**Should Decline If.** No real AI task to build during the sprint; the real block is the
model's capability ceiling, not specification; the constraint is actually evaluation
discipline (A2), not specification.

### A2 — No Evaluation Discipline

**Why it matters.** Without evals, AI work is faith-based — the operator can't trust, defend,
or improve the system, and it breaks silently in production. The ability to define a
falsifiable quality bar and gate changes against it separates a serious AI operator from
someone who ships on vibes.

**Failure Mode.** Quality is judged by eyeballing one example; no test cases; no regression
check; "looks good" is the only standard; quality claims are unfalsifiable.

**The Bar.** Output quality is defined by a falsifiable eval (cases + pass criteria), and
changes are checked against it, not vibes.

**Difficulty Ladder.**
- **Rung 1 — Pass criteria written:** a real output has explicit pass/fail criteria.
- **Rung 2 — Eval set built:** a set of real cases with pass criteria exists and runs.
- **Rung 3 — Catches a regression, unaided:** the eval catches a real regression you'd
  otherwise have shipped; you build an eval for a new task cold.
- **Rung 4 — Gates production, peer-trusted:** a real eval suite gates a workflow, accepted by
  a technical peer as a genuine quality bar.

**Example Reps.**
- **Day 1:** Write pass/fail criteria for one real AI output; baseline.
- **Intermediate:** Build a small eval (a handful of real cases) for a real task.
- **Independence:** Use the eval to catch a real regression; build an eval for a new task,
  unaided.
- **Proof:** Ship a real workflow gated by an eval a technical peer trusts.

**Proof.** A real eval suite that gates an AI workflow, accepted by a technical peer as a real
quality bar.

**Recognition.** Technical peers and whoever ships the product see AI quality defined and
gated by a falsifiable eval — not eyeballed — the eval suite is the marker.

**Should Decline If.** No real AI output or workflow to evaluate during the sprint; the real
block is no ground truth or labels are available (a circumstance); the constraint is actually
specification (A1) upstream.

**Ranking logic.** Test 7 (Replaceability) and Test 1 (Validity) are decisive, so the top of
the list favors **judgment-and-ownership capabilities a competent tool cannot do and a pricer
visibly rewards** — turning analysis into decisions (DA1), making a technical decision legible
(E1), writing transformation rather than features (C1), defining falsifiable success (P3),
and tying design to decisions (D1). Constraints whose *output* an AI can already draft (copy,
SEO) rank slightly lower on **Scarcity** unless the bar targets the irreducibly human
judgment layer (the controlling idea, the reader's transformation, intent-fit) — which these
bars deliberately do. Items lower on the list are strong capabilities whose **recognizability**
or **30-day feedback horizon** is marginally weaker and must be watched at design time.

**Governance.** Promote a candidate to an authored constraint only after it passes §9 of the
Manual; promote to `active_v1: true` only by a deliberate decision. Retire any constraint that
real Sprint data shows fails Test 6 (no transfer) or Test 7 (no effect on replaceability).
